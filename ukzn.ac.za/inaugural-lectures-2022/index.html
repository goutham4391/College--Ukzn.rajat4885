Inaugural lectures present an opportunity for showcasing exciting and ground-breaking research and teaching being carried out by professors in our University. Each lecture represents a significant milestone in an academic’s career, providing official recognition of their promotion or appointment to full professorship. These lectures are furthermore an ideal opportunity for new professors to introduce themselves and to present an overview of their own contribution to their field to academic peers, students and research collaborators. They are also a platform for celebrating a professor’s academic achievements with his/her family, friends, mentors and colleagues.

Inaugural lectures form part of the University’s Public lectures series and may only be given by all newly appointed full professors who have been appointed in academic Schools and Centres.

Professor Colleen Aldous

Qualifications: B.Sc, B.Sc (Hons), M.Sc (UP), PhD (University of Twente, The Netherlands)

Title: Evidence-based Medicine: A Humanitarian Endeavour or Number Crunching Effort? What Does It Mean for Our Research?
 
Date: 21 November 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

The practice of medicine is subject to many influences that challenge the ethical and scientific fundamentals of achieving good health and saving lives. Historically, medical practitioners follow a combination of study and practice including mentorship, and personal and anecdotal evidence, that are aligned with ethical principles. Evidence-based medicine emerged alongside this process to ensure scientific rigour for medical decision-making. But this process has evolved into processes of dominance through profit-driven models of evidence that have overwhelmed individual practice, leading to a contest between elitist models of research and ethics and practitioners who have the immediate interests of their patients in closest view. If evidence-based medicine and human research ethics developed as a result of medical research atrocities of the twentieth century, the COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the need for scientific integrity in this century.

CV

Professor Colleen Aldous is a Full Professor in the School of Clinical Medicine, College of Health Sciences, University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN), Durban, South Africa.

After a career as a school teacher and focussing on motherhood, Professor Aldous returned to university to receive a B.Sc (Honors) in Genetics and an M.Sc in Science Education (Genetics) in the Faculty of Natural and Agricultural Sciences at the University of Pretoria in 2002 and 2005, respectively. She was conferred her doctorate in science education at the Univerity of Twente in the Netherlands in 2009.

Professor Aldous joined the Department of Genetics on the Pietermaritzburg Campus of the Univerity of Natal in 2004, as a contract lecturer. After moving to Durban and working as a post-doc at CAPRISA and HIV Pathogenesis Program (HPP),  she joined the then Faculty of Medicine in 2010 as a research coordinator. After the university restructuring, she was allocated to the School of Clinical Medicine School as a Senior Lecturer and was promoted to Associate Professor in 2015 and Full Professor in 2020.

Professor Aldous’s research area remains in the field of genetics but has expanded to include investigations into postgraduate education in the clinical setting. In her role as a research support professor, she supports research across all clinical disciplines. This global view of clinical research has led her to think about the field of Evidence-Based Medicine, particularly its interpretations and communication to the public.

She has supervised a senior doctorate in obstetrics and gynaecology and 18 Doctoral (PhD) studies in genetics, general surgery, orthopaedic surgery, plastic surgery, psychology, podiatry, anaesthetics, internal medicine, and paediatrics. She has further supervised 14 MMed and seven MMedsci students and mentored two post-docs. She is currently leading 20 PhD candidates in various clinical disciplines. Professor Aldous has been a mentor on several platforms, including DRILL and the international King’s College London Mentorship Program run by Dr Tom Rusbridge.

With her students and collaborators, as well as independently, she has published over 150 research articles in indexed journals and several in the lay press. She has written chapters for books, both in textbooks and in peer-reviewed books and written and edited a book on protocol writing aimed at the MMed student. She has been the recipient of the UKZN Top 30 award twice.

As an academic, she has taken her role in the public understanding of science seriously. She was interviewed on several radio stations, national television, and newspapers during the COVID-19 pandemic, focussing on research and evidence-based medicine.

Professor Aldous has examined several postgraduate theses for other universities and has reviewed articles for many academic journals. She has been a guest speaker at national and international conferences and has been part of the organisation of both national and international conferences.

Professor Aldous was elected a Member of the Academy of Sciences of South Africa in 2021.

Title: A Cosmic Odyssey
 
Date: 18 November 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

In this talk, I will review my academic journey through cosmology, the study of the large-scale structure and evolution of the universe, and pay tribute to teachers and mentors that have guided me on this journey. En route, I have had early encounters with the theory of general relativity and developed models for the origin of small fluctuations in the early universe that seeded the cosmic structure we see around us. I have traversed the path of constraining these cosmological models, using data from measurements of the cosmic microwave background, to most recently being involved in building a telescope in South Africa that will measure the large-scale distribution of hydrogen in the universe and thereby uncover the properties of the mysterious dark energy that pervades our universe. Over the course of this talk, I will provide an overview of the state of modern cosmology and how we have arrived at the cosmological model, driven by principles of simplicity, but I will also reflect on our role as scientists in society, and the inherent complexity that this entails.

CV

Kavilan is currently full professor in the School of Mathematics, Statistics and Computer Science, and director of the Astrophysics Research Centre, at the University of KwaZulu-Natal. He completed his PhD in 2002 at the University of Cambridge, after which he worked as a postdoctoral researcher in astrophysics at the University of Oxford, before returning to South Africa in 2003 to take up an academic faculty position at UKZN. Kavilan’s research interest is in the area of cosmology and involves confronting cosmological theories with observational data, in particular observations of the cosmic microwave background and large-scale structure. Kavilan served as a scientific board member of the Atacama Cosmology Telescope project, a member of the international science working group for the Square Kilometre Array Project, and as associate editor for the South African Journal of Science. In 2007, Kavilan won the South African Institute of Physics Silver Jubilee Medal, in 2011 he was awarded a Fulbright Visiting Research Scholar (VRS) Fellowship to Princeton University, and in 2022 he was awarded a Fulbright VRS Fellowship to the Flatiron Institute at the Simons Foundation in New York. He served as a member of the NRF Astronomy Advisory Council and the SARAO Science Advisory Committee, and in 2020 was elected to the Academy of Science of South Africa. Kavilan has published 110 research papers with over 8500 citations and has delivered invited lectures at a number of international conferences, including the International Dark Side of the Universe Conference in 2014 and the COSPAR Scientific Assembly in 2018. He is currently the principal investigator of the HIRAX telescope project, which aims to measure the evolution of dark energy and localise fast radio bursts.

Title: What Makes Ecosystems Resilient and Persist over Time?
 
Date: 3 November 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

What makes ecosystems resilient and persist over time?

Biodiversity shapes ecological systems by causing a variety of complexities, which allow the ecosystem to adapt to external and internal changes in the short and long term. The degree of species interactions, species’ roles and functions determine ecosystem responses to change, where biodiversity can be an insurance for resilience and impact on certain ecosystem services. Systems ecology provides a holistic view of ecological systems and their biodiversities, enabling us to examine direct and indirect processes which also facilitate ecosystem services. Thus, understanding the link between biodiversity, functional roles, and ecosystem behaviour is paramount to comprehend the resilience and persistence of ecosystems over time, and this is illustrated from both a reductionist and a systems approach. How to ensure the continued existence of different biodiversities and the provision of ecosystem services by estuarine and marine ecosystems depends on our understanding of the underlying processes, and on how to best convey complex adaptive behaviour to managers.

CV

Professor Ursula Scharler joined UKZN in August 2006 as a Lecturer after completing post-doctoral studies at the Chesapeake Biological Laboratory (MD, USA), the Smithsonian Institute (MD, USA) and Wageningen University (The Netherlands). Her main fields of research are ecology, systems analysis and ecosystem modelling on estuarine, marine and socio-economic systems, with an emphasis on variability, resilience, extinctions, inter- and transdisciplinarity. She holds several Associate Editor positions with international journals, and engages in advisory committees, as member and chair. She has organised and participated in many international and national workshops, the latest in 2022 on ecosystem indicators and as a member of a UN advisory seeking interlinkages between Sustainable Development Goals. Professor Scharler regularly reviews national and international grant applications, and for national and international journals. She has graduated several MSc and PhD students, and supervised post-doctoral fellows. Professor Scharler currently enjoys a NRF B2 rating, and was invited keynote speaker to 6 international conferences.

Title: Herbal Teas as a Strategic Focus Area for the National Industry Policy Framework: Potential Prospects for Future Industrialisation
 
Date: 18 October 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

The inaugural lecture will cover the work on herbal tea production, biochemistry of herbal bush tea in a research which has been done over the period of 20 year in South Africa. Bush tea (Athrixia phylicoides DC.) is an aromatic perennial shrub that has medicinal properties. There are 14 species of bush tea and 9 of these are found in South Africa. This shrub has leafy stems that grow up to a metre in height. The plant belongs to the Asteraceae family. Bush tea grows in rocky areas, grasslands and forests across South Africa. Traditionally, bush tea has been used in cleansing blood, treating headaches, boils, bad acne, sore throat and loss of voice. Various tribes and cultures in South Africa use bush tea in various ways for different purposes, for instances Vhavenda people use bush tea as an aphrodisiac and Sothos also use strong brew as a calming wash for sore feet. Zulu people in KwaZulu-Natal use dried stems of bush tea to make brooms. Bush tea tea is rich in non-toxic flavonoids, antioxidants and tannin levels are very low, making it a favourable healthy beverage. Moreover, the absence of caffeine is a desirable feature of a healthy beverage. The plants have compounds which has been discovered and characterised for the first time. The details of its biochemistry, agronomic practices medicinal properties will be discussed.

Title: “Territories in Flow and Flux”: Anthropological Knowledge and Virtual Communities
 
Date: 12 October 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

The phenomenon of migration continues to take twists and turns in contemporary times. Coupled with new technologies, human movements continue to be reshaped in novel ways and this has implications for anthropology as a discipline, particularly for the anthropological method. Perhaps one can toy with the idea of virtual societies which are emerging out of the technological interaction of migrants who share common interests and identities. Mobility continues to reshape anthropological discourse and although it appears to engender greater freedoms, my major critique centres on the globalization of inequality which is characteristic of the present migration trends. These are affected by technological developments which have heightened the degree of surveillance and has implications for inclusion and exclusion. If the modern anthropologist’s duty is to study people and their relationships everywhere, the transnational connections and virtual communities which people are part of should be interesting perspectives that need to be understood.

Title: Sustainable rural learning ecologies: A critical analysis
 
Date: 5 October 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

My most important contribution to existing knowledge and research is carving a research area called ‘sustainable rural learning ecologies’. I coined this area through enmeshing issues of sustainability [as informed by SDGs], rurality [my residential, social, and employment geographies for over five decades], learning [a rudimentary, prominent and pervasive phenomenon in my field of specialisation] as well as ‘ecologies’ which I took from geophysical/natural/positivistic interpretation to social/humanistic/interpretive paradigm. My contribution further enriched Barron (2004, 2006) notion of learning ecology. I adopted the learning ecology framework which contends that learning takes place within (intra), across (inter), and between spaces such as schools and the various entities of the communities. One of the concerns I raise relates to rural schools, colleges and universities that seem to, unintentionally so, structure their learning programmes in such a manner that they neglect rural attributes and resultantly ostracize or marginalize learners/students from rural environments. To complete the loop, these institutions are more likely to fail in preparing graduates for decisive contribution in sustainable rural learning ecologies.

Title: Saving the Environment – using the little things
 
Date: 29 September 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

Advancement in science and biotechnology have progressively aided mankind through   exploration and use of natural resources. Unfortunately, some of these interventions may instigate environmental effects which impacts human health.

Natural bioremediation is an ecologically sound tool that encompasses the usage of biological processes to reduce or remove pollutants through biochemical solubilization or mineralization. Evidence emanating from the Exxon Valdez and Deepwater Horizon oil spills reveal microbes playing significant roles in restoring ecological health. Hydrocarbon degrading microbial isolates possess the potential for innovation in many applications.

Combinations of advantaged molecular techniques, molecular modelling together with artificial intelligence potentiates bioremediation for commercialization and environmental health that can be demonstrated in plastic degradation.

This lecture is an opportunity to share my research in this area.

Title: Thin film polymer solar cells as an alternative source of renewable energy:
Progress and Challenges
 
Date: 20 September 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

Conducting polymers have attracted tremendous research interest in the last three decades because of their potential application in the area of flexible photonic and electronic devices. Thin-film polymer solar cell (TFPSC) is one of the highly sought area of application with the view to produce light weight and flexible solar panel. The energy band structure of the donor and acceptor molecules, in the preparation of polymers blend bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) solar absorber medium, is of great importance to the overall performance of polymer solar cell. The limitation in tuning the energy band structures of fullerene molecules poses significant challenges to the progress in BHJ polymer solar cells. In an attempt to cater the challenges, the small molecules non-fullerene acceptors (NFAs) came into the TFPSC research space with the possibility of altering the optoelectronic properties the polymer molecules that brought polymer solar cells closer to the realization of full-scale commercialization. The use of plasmon metal nanoparticles have contributed to ease some of the limitations of polymer molecules. Opportunities and challenges of TFPSC in contributing towards the realization of cheap renewable energy will be presented.

CV

Professor Genene Tessema Mola is a Full Professor in the Discipline of Physics, College of Agriculture Engineering and Science, University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN), Pietermaritzburg, South Africa.

Professor GT Mola received B.Sc. and MSc Physics Degree from Addis Ababa University, Ethiopia. He obtained PhD Degrees from the University of Bonn, Germany in 2003.

Professor GT Mola has served several academic institutions as full-time academic staff since his graduation from Addis Ababa University and University of Bonn, respectively. He was member of academic staff of the Department of Physics at Alemaya University, Addis Ababa University and National University of Lesotho.  In January 2011, he joined the School of Chemistry & Physics, Physics Cluster, UKZN, Pietermaritzburg, South Africa as an Associate Professor of Physics and then promoted to the position of Full Professor in 2020.

Professor GT Mola’s research area is Experimental Condensed Matter Physics. Abstract

Title: Missing Data Within the Ambit of Flexible Statistical Modelling
 
Date: 26 July 2022
 
Time: 15h00 – 16h00
Abstract

Even in a well-designed and controlled study, missing data occurs in almost all research. Missing data can reduce the statistical power of a study and can produce biased estimates, leading to invalid conclusions. This warrants the need to review problems and types of missing data, along with the techniques for handling missing data. The mechanisms by which missing data occurs are illustrated, and the methods for handling the missing data are interrogated within the ambit of flexible statistical modelling. The classical methods range from complete case analysis (CC), last observation carried forward (LOCF) and mean value imputation whilst the more modern techniques include Multiple Imputation (MI), Multiple Imputation via Chained Equation( MICE), Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) and Multiple Subset Correspondence analysis. The merits and demerits of the aforementioned techniques are also discussed.

Title: Seemingly Seamless? Roughage Intake by Ruminants
 
Date: 23 June 2022

Abstract

Seemingly seamless is the nature of diet intake by ruminants; it captures some salient features of research by Professor Ignatius V Nsahlai. The discussion starts with two models on (1) digestible organic matter (DOM) required as by sheep in the tropical highlands and (2) his published methodology for estimating DOM intake of grazing livestock. His group further proposed a model for estimating methane emission where the level of feeding was in multiples of reticulo-rumen capacity. This model out-performed its immediate contemporary (R2 80.5 vs 60.5%). One enigma in diet formulation is the unknown daily intake. His research group developed a model using a multiplicative strategy for estimating the daily intake of diets by ruminants.  In view of some shortcomings, his research group has tested and approved the model by Illius and Gordon based on its structure but the input parameters are being re-modelled. Is the research really seamless?

Title: A Turbulent Learning Curve: Technology Integration

Date: 15 June 2022

Time: 15h00 – 16h00

Abstract

The integration of technology is a continuous challenge in most organizations and industry. A recent survey, post the onset of covid-19, shows that Emerging technology adoption has a people problem and therefore one needs to get out of one’s own way to embrace change. This is true for the education sector as well.

As early as 2007, it became evident that, regardless of the amount of technology and its sophistication, technology will not be used appropriately unless educators have the skills, knowledge, and positive attitudes towards its use.

More than 15 years later, the same holds true and many are still researching technology adoption in education. How do we explain this phenomenon? Moreover, with ongoing technological developments, and the classroom having evolved into a seabed of technological advances, the big idea for education is finally here: How do we facilitate an exploration of new education approaches that reflect today’s students and 21st century skills?
